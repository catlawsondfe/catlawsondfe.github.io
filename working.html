What you should do before you work with external organisations

Using external organisations can help you to:

  • bring in specialist knowledge and expertise
  • provide authentic personal or professional perspectives
  • establish a positive first contact for pupils with wider agencies such as police or health providers

You should only use external organisations to enhance teaching and not as a replacement for teaching by appropriate teaching staff.

Make sure safeguarding measures are in place before you work with any external organisations, including checking credentials and the suitability of content in advance.

Before inviting external organisations to your school, you should make your visitors aware of any policies and procedures they will need to follow.

Example from a secondary, mixed academy based in a rural area

There is a school-wide policy for anybody external coming into the school. This means a pre-check before the agency comes in, looking at safeguarding etc. and also have conversations beforehand about the content of the workshops. We’ll have a dialogue afterwards if any concerns.

Challenges and solutions

Making sure content is consistent with the planned curriculum

You should check that content delivered by an external organisation matches your school’s curriculum aims. You may find that ad hoc sessions have little value if they are not reinforced by subsequent lessons.

Solution

Meeting the external organisation beforehand to co-plan their input may help with this. It also provides an ideal opportunity to clarify that pupils must never be encouraged to make personal disclosures in front of their peers in class.

Visitors from external agencies may have topic expertise but are not necessarily experts in teaching. It is important that teachers advise and support visitors to plan sessions that achieve the intended outcomes, including conveying the intended knowledge effectively.

Example from a secondary, mixed, local authority maintained school based in a rural area

The PSHE lead ensures that visitors “have to meet with me to show me and walk me through what they are going to deliver before I let them deliver anything”.

Example from a secondary, mixed academy based in a rural area

The PSHE lead explained that “I have a phone conversation in advance regarding the content to be covered and what [the visitor and I] want to achieve”. This establishes a shared approach that ensures materials are appropriate and content relevant to their planned programme. They added, “I warn the organisation about any issues that might come up with particular pupils, and we discuss common questions to expect from pupils.”

Getting the tone right

Check external organisations’ content for:

  • graphic images or stories which may upset vulnerable pupils
  • content which may inadvertently promote harmful behaviour, for example, detailing or describing methods of self-harm

Solution

External organisations can only make meaningful input to the curriculum if their session has been co-designed, or at the very least reviewed, before delivery.

This review should check that content:

  • is age-appropriate
  • meets the intended aims of the lesson
  • matches pupils’ needs
  • meets best practice principles

Check safeguarding knowledge and understanding

Schools identified concerns related to external organisations’ or speakers’ understanding of safeguarding best practice.

For example, School A identified that the organisation “was happy to discuss and disclose information that the school was not”.

Solution

Make sure all visitors are clear on safeguarding policies and procedures before entering the classroom. It is essential visitors approach sensitive topics safely, including avoidance of ‘shock tactics’ that might distress pupils or give unintended messages.

Do a background check in advance to ensure visitors provide unbiased, accurate information and are transparent about their intentions and funding sources.

Finding and choosing visitors

School C identified that it was difficult to find external agencies “as there are not many agencies out there that make themselves available”. School B raised the cost implications: “Lots of agencies who have impressive offers charge enormous amounts” and that “not every agency is good and not every [provider] is good”.

Solution

To save costs, you could try and use voluntary organisations and local services such as:

  • local police
  • the fire service
  • local charities

A school may on expert knowledge from the police and lawyers to develop their teaching about the law and consent, as well as child exploitation.

A mixed primary SEND school in an urban area uses “St John Ambulance and the Red Cross to do a lesson around safety and first aid”.

Further guidance and support

Read the statutory guidance on relationships education, relationships and sex education (RSE) and health education. Page 18 covers information on working with external agencies.

UKCCIS has guidance on using external visitors to support online safety education.
