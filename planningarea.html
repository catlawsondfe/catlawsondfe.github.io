Setting your pupil planning area

How planning areas should be structured for the school capacity survey and how to request changes to your structure.

You must make sure that pupil planning area data reported for your school capacity survey return is a true and accurate reflection of the need for school places.

We may defer the decision about a local authorityâ€™s basic need allocation until we can be confident that the data submitted represents a true and accurate reflection of the need for school places.

How pupil planning areas should be structured

Planning areas should be structured separately for primary and secondary phases. It is usual for local authorities to have multiple planning areas.

You should structure planning areas to minimise the number of changes so they remain stable for at least 5 years.

For example, where you know that parental preference often changes between a group of schools, grouping these schools in one planning area may prevent a future change to the planning area structures.

What to include in your planning area structure

Group together in one pupil planning area, schools which are located close together and which pupils could reasonably attend.

Individual planning areas should contain existing school capacity - there should be at least one open school located within them.

For the purposes of the school capacity survey, planning areas:

  • that do not have existing school capacity will not be approved
  • must contain more than one school
  • should be structured to minimise the need for future changes
  • should remain fixed for at least 5 years unless there are compelling reasons to change
  • should include all school places up to year 6 (primary) and all places from year 7 (secondary)

If you operate a 3-tier education system, do not separate the first, middle and upper phased schools into separate planning areas.

You should make sure all sites of split-site schools are contained within the same planning area. If we agree that split sites can be contained within different planning areas, you may need to provide additional information for each site for your return.

Proposals for single school planning areas will only be approved where you provide clear evidence that it would be unreasonable to group the school with others in the authority.

Selective schools, UTCs, faith or free schools

This also applies to requests to locate single schools with discrete characteristics, such as selective schools, UTCs, faith or free schools, in their own single school planning areas. You should also avoid grouping these schools into separate planning areas overlaid on top of others of the same educational phase.

For example, avoid grouping all faith schools in one planning area, regardless of geography, as this can result in the need for places being inaccurately reported.

Forecasts of pupil numbers in the planning areas containing schools with discrete characteristics should be capped at current capacity unless there is a shortfall of places in all appropriate underlying planning areas.

Number of schools in planning area structures

The accuracy of the forecasts collected via the school capacity survey is crucial to:

  • assess shortfalls and surpluses in school capacity
  • inform the calculation of basic need capital allocations to support new pupil places.

In general, the larger the number of schools in the planning area, the more accurate the forecast. Planning areas should be an appropriate size for the area to make sure that place pressure is neither masked nor over-represented.

The number of schools in a planning area should be determined by whether the schools in the planning area represent a reasonable alternative to each other. For example, pupils who attend one school could also reasonably be expected to attend another in the planning area.

It is recommended that planning areas for:

  • primary schools contain a minimum of 4 schools but ideally contain 6 or more schools
  • secondary schools contain a minimum of 2 schools

In many cases, planning areas should be considerably larger than this, where the schools represent a reasonable alternative to each other.

We understand that the different geographical characteristics of local authorities may make it difficult for some local authorities to have secondary planning areas with a larger number of schools.

Reviewing planning area structures

Check the structure of your planning areas regularly to make sure they reflect any changes that have taken place or will shortly take place such as:

  • housing developments
  • schools opening or closing
  • infrastructure changes, for example, new roads

If you identify a different planning area structure is essential, you can propose changes within the 5 years.

When reviewing planning area structures, consider:

  • geographical characteristics
  • the distance to the nearest school
  • parental preference patterns

Our checks on planning area structures

Every year we review each local authoritiesâ€™ planning area structures in advance of the school capacity survey. data collection. This is to make sure:

  • planning areas are fit for purpose
  • there is consistency across authorities of similar size and geographical location or characteristics

When identifying local authorities whose planning area structures are to be prioritised to review we use a range of criteria. This includes looking at local authorities:

  • with a lower forecast accuracy than other local authorities with similar socio-economic characteristics (statistical neighbours), over a number of years
  • with planning area(s) that are not geographically isolated and contain fewer than the recommended minimum number of schools
  • whose structures do not correspond with one or more of the general principles
  • who have historically not increased capacity in line with basic need capital allocations
  • which actually plan and deliver places on a different basis from the planning areas they use for the school capacity survey
  • which have regularly required changes to planning areas outside of the changes which do not require our approval

We will contact the local authorities we have identified to discuss their planning area structure. You will need to supply:

  • proposals for changes to the planning area structure to bring it into line with the guidance, with robust evidence to support this
  • or where the local authority considers that changes to the planning areas would be detrimental, robust evidence to support the structures of planning areas within the authority
  • where the proposed structure is based on catchment areas and travel distances:
    • robust data detailing this catchment data and supply and demand on places.
    • maps illustrating the planning area structure to be adopted
    • information on the distances between schools
  • where the proposed structure includes any single school planning areas:
    • an explanation as to why this is the case
    • evidence of catchment data for schoolsâ€™ intake years
    • details of other schools in close proximity
  • maps or data showing the flow of pupils which live in one planning area and attend schools in another

We will assess the evidence you provide to:

  • determine whether the proposed planning area structure is appropriate
  • check youâ€™re over estimating or under-estimating when reporting a need for additional places

Local authorities which have agreed a planning area structure after a review will not be prioritised again for review again for at least 5 years, unless essential.

Changing current pupil planning area structures

If you want to structurally change or create pupil planning areas for use in the school capacity survey return, you need to get approval from us.

You can request changes to planning areas annually.

What you need to send us

If you want to change the structure of your planning area, you need to send us:

  • supporting documentation to explain the construction of and rationale for the proposed planning areas within the authority
  • details of where the proposed structure would take the local authority outside the recommendations on the minimum number of schools in a planning area and a clear explanation for this.
  • forecasts of pupil numbers for all the planning areas which are affected by the proposed changes
  • a statement detailing the methodology used for the forecasts
  • a clear rationale supported by documentation for each other element of the planning area structure which does not follow this guidance
  • maps or data showing the flow of pupils who live in one planning area and attend schools in another

If the structure is framed around catchment areas and travel distances, you will also need to send us:

  • data (including mapping information) detailing this catchment data and the supply of and demand for places
  • mapping information on the distances between schools

If the proposed planning area only has one school, you will also need to explain:

  • why this is the case
  • why it would be unreasonable to expect pupils to travel to the next nearest school
  • if the situation has changed from the previous year
  • the catchment data for intake years
  • which alternative schools are in close proximity

Approvals for changes to pupil planning area structures

We have to approve changes to the structure of planning areas before the school capacity survey.

This does not include changes to individual school details that do not affect the structure of planning areas such as changing the schoolâ€™s age range. You are required to let us know about these changes via the annual checking of the schools list.

You can change the structure of a planning area without DfE approval only where:

  • a school previously opened in temporary accommodation is moving to a permanent site which is located in a different planning area to the temporary accommodation
  • a new school has opened, in which case the local authority should assign it to the appropriate planning area
  • a school has closed (unless it results in a single school planning area or a planning area with no existing open school capacity)

What weâ€™ll check

We will then analyse your rationale and evidence to determine whether the proposals are appropriate and do not overestimate or underestimate the need for additional school places.

We consider a range of factors when reaching a decision, including whether the proposed:

  • planning area structure seems reasonable compared to other local authorities that are statistical neighbours
  • changes will create single school planning areas
  • changes will create planning areas with fewer schools than the recommended minimum number of schools
  • planning area has existing schools - we will not approve the creation of a planning area if there are no open schools in the proposed area
  • planning area boundaries appear reasonable, considering the location of rivers, roads and railway lines
  • planning areas likely to require changing again within the next 5 years

We will also analyse existing school capacity survey data to check if the proposed changes will overestimate or underestimate the need for school places.

Next steps

We will contact you to let you know the outcome of your request.

If weâ€™re unable to reach an agreement on an existing or proposed planning area structure, we may propose regrouping planning areas differently. We will also explain why the existing or proposed planning area structure is unsuitable.

Key dates for changing pupil planning areas

Changes to planning area structures affect the data you need to submit for the annual school capacity survey. This means we need to approve your proposed planning area changes in advance of the next school capacity survey collection.

September

We will ask you to confirm your current planning area structure is correct by mid to late September.

If you want to make changes, you will need to provide:

  • a clear explanation of the changes
  • the reasons behind the proposals
  • supporting documentation, for example, maps

October

We will continue to work with local authorities :

  • which have been identified as priorities for a planning area review to propose new planning areas
  • requesting changes to provide any further supporting document required

November

We will let you know whether proposals have been rejected, deferred or are being fully reviewed.

December and January

A full review of the proposals takes place. We may ask local authorities proposing to provide further explanation of the changes, the reasons behind the proposals and supporting documentation.

February

Local authorities can make any changes to planning areas or schools that do not need approval.

We will let you know whether your proposed changes to your planning areas have been approved. If not approved, we may propose regrouping planning areas differently.

March

Local authorities have to return the final list of planning areas and schools correctly assigned in each area by end of March.
